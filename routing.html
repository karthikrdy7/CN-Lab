Switching gets a frame across one local network. Routing is what happens between different networks — routers pass a packet from one to the next, each one making an independent decision about the best next hop, until it reaches its destination. Pick a source and destination router below.

Why the numbers on the links matter

Each link between routers has a cost — often based on bandwidth or latency. Routers don't just pick the path with the fewest hops; they pick the path with the lowest total cost, even if that means passing through more routers. This simulation uses Dijkstra's algorithm, one of the real algorithms link-state routing protocols like OSPF are built on.

If a link goes down, routers detect it and recalculate — this is why the internet can keep working even when a cable gets cut somewhere in the middle. Traffic just finds a different path.
